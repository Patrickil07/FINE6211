The Role of the Financial Manager

An Interactive Summary

Learning Outcome 1: Primary Roles of the Financial Manager

The financial manager plays a crucial role in the success of any organization. Their responsibilities can be broken down into three core functions. Hover over the cards to learn more about each function.

Financial Analysis & Planning

Involves evaluating the firm's financial health, analyzing financial statements, and creating budgets and forecasts to guide future financial activities.

Making Investment Decisions

Also known as Capital Budgeting, this involves selecting projects and assets to invest in that will generate sufficient returns and contribute to the firm's value.

Making Financing Decisions

This involves determining the optimal mix of debt and equity (Capital Structure) to fund the firm's operations and investments at the lowest possible cost.

Learning Outcome 2: The Balancing Act

Financial managers must perform a delicate balancing act between returns, cash distributions, and risk to maximize shareholder wealth. Click the tabs below to explore each component.

Timing of Returns

Shareholders expect returns through dividends or share price appreciation. Managers must decide whether to distribute cash now or reinvest it for potentially higher future returns, balancing immediate gratification with long-term growth.

Cash Distributions

The main methods are dividends and share repurchases. Regular dividends signal stability, but reduce cash for reinvestment. Share repurchases can boost share price but may be less reliable in the eyes of investors.

Prevalent and Pending Risks

Managers must identify, assess, and mitigate various risks (market, credit, operational). Taking too much risk can jeopardize the firm, while being too conservative can lead to missed opportunities. The goal is to maximize risk-adjusted returns.

Learning Outcome 3: Ethical Considerations

Ethics are the foundation of a financial manager's conduct. Explore the key ethical pillars below.

Financial managers have a legal and ethical obligation to act in the best interests of their clients or shareholders. This is the bedrock of trust in the financial system.

Ethical conduct demands complete, accurate, and timely disclosure of financial information. Misleading stakeholders is a severe breach of ethics.

Managers must identify, disclose, and manage any potential conflicts of interest fairly and transparently, always prioritizing corporate interests over personal gain.

An ethical approach focuses on sustainable, long-term value creation for all stakeholders, rather than just maximizing short-term profits.

Learning Outcome 4: The Agency Problem

The agency problem arises from the separation of ownership (shareholders) and control (managers) in a corporation.

Core Conflict

The interests of the manager (agent) may not always align with the interests of the shareholders (principal), leading to potential self-serving behavior.

Agency Costs

These are the costs shareholders bear to mitigate the agency problem. Click to reveal the different types of costs.

Costs incurred by shareholders to oversee managers' actions, such as audit fees and expenses for the board of directors.

Costs incurred by managers to assure shareholders they are acting in their best interests, such as performance-based compensation.

The reduction in shareholder wealth that still occurs due to misaligned interests, even after monitoring and bonding costs are incurred.
